Adjectives of quantity

Adjectives which shows the quantity of nouns or pronouns.

Adjectives of quantity show how much of a thing is meant; as — some, little, enough, no, much, etc.

Adjectives of Quantity are always followed by a Singular Noun and this Noun must always be either a Noun of Material or an Abstract Noun otherwise they would be quantifier pronouns.

Difference between "any" and "some"

Some is used in positive sentences, for countable and uncountable nouns.

Any is used in questions and sentences that are grammatically positive but are meant to be negative. There is an exception to this as the word some can also be used for interrogative sentences or demands.

  • My mother asked me to buy some class="c4"> oranges.
  • I shall not buy any class="c4"> oranges (Negative).
  • Has Sammy bought any class="c4"> apples? (Interrogative)
  • Can I borrow some colors for my drawing? (Interrogative).

Differences between "much" and "many"

Much is used with uncountable nouns.

Many is used with countable nouns.

  • There was much joy when we heard the good news that we were being paid much money.
  • There are many people living in the many houses on that street

Difference between "little" and "few"

Little can only describe non-countable nouns.

Few should be used only to modify countable nouns.

  • Because there was so little fire, people noticed little smoke.
  • She made very few hamburgers, so she only was able to sell a few hamburgers.
