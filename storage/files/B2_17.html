Adjectives ending in “-ING

How ING adjectives are formed

The ING adjective is formed by adding ing to the base form of the verb:

interest > interesting

This ING form is called the present participle. We are using it as an adjective.

Example:

I watched an interesting film.

That is a confusing situation

(Auxiliary verb + Adjective + ing suffix)

Common Adjectives ending With ING (Meaning and Sentences)

Amusing

an amusing story/person/situation

  • It's a very amusing play with an uproarious final act.
  • He told one or two amusing anecdotes about his years as a policeman.
  • I was listening to an amusing program on the radio, which made me laugh out loud.

Annoying

making you feel slightly angry:

  • It's really annoying when a train is late and there's no explanation.
  • He has a really annoying laugh.
  • It's annoying that we can't travel until Thursday, but at least the fare's cheaper then.

Boring

not interesting or exciting:

  • Sometimes I enjoy my English lessons, but at other times I find them really boring.
  • Your hair has a natural wave whereas mine's just straight and boring.
  • This town is so boring in the evening - there's never anything happening.
  • Life would be bloody boring if nothing ever went wrong.

Confusing

Something that is confusing makes you feel confused because it is difficult to understand:

  • We have two people called Paul James working here, so it's a little confusing!
  • The instructions are really confusing. Could you help me with them, please?
  • The transition from boyhood to manhood can be a confusing period.
  • It was so confusing that eventually it got to the point where no one knew what was going on.

Disappointing

making you feel disappointed.

  • What a disappointing result!
  • The response to our advertisement has been somewhat disappointing.
  • Tiger Woods is determined to erase the memory of a disappointing Cup debut two years ago.
  • The company blamed its disappointing performance on a misguided business plan.

Depressing

making you feel unhappy and without hope for the future.

  • This weather is so depressing.
  • Her letter made depressing reading.
  • Everywhere looks so gray and depressing in winter.
  • I find the hopelessness of the situation very depressing.
  • It's depressing to realize how quickly you get old.

Exciting

making you feel excited

  • She led an exciting and glamorous life.
  • It was the most exciting holiday I've ever had.
  • Flying is exciting the first time you do it, but you soon become jaded.
  • It was one of the most exciting moments in his life.

Frustrating

making you feel annoyed or less confident because you cannot achieve what you want

  • He doesn't listen to what I say and it's so frustrating.
  • I guess job opportunities at the top are improving for women, but it’s such a slow process that it’s frustrating.
  • We spent a frustrating five minutes while the pilot warmed up the engines.
  • I find it frustrating that I can't speak other languages.

Frightening

making you feel fear.

  • The mask was so lifelike it was quite frightening.
  • The deathly pallor of her skin was frightening.
  • She found the film extremely frightening.
  • The firefighters had a frightening task ahead of them.
  • There's a frightening similarity between this incident and one that occurred last year.

Interesting

Someone or something that is interesting keeps your attention because he, she, or it is unusual, exciting, or has a lot of ideas.

  • She's quite an interesting woman.
  • She has some very interesting things to say on the subject.
  • It is always interesting to hear other people's point of view.
  • How can we make science lessons more interesting?
  • I'm always looking for interesting new recipes.

Shocking

offensive, upsetting, or immoral

  • There are few crimes more truly shocking than the murder or abuse of children.
  • The city's most shocking statistic is its high infant mortality rate.
  • He gave a very vivid and often shocking account of his time in prison.
  • The motive for this shocking attack seems to be racial hatred.

Surprising

Unexpected

  • After a six-hour flight and a long day of meetings, it's not surprising you conked out.
  • If you feed your dog cakes and biscuits, it's not surprising he's so fat.
  • After a grueling six months singing on a world tour, it is hardly surprising that her voice is starting to go.
  • A surprising percentage of the population are illiterate.
  • My inability to do needlework was treated with surprising indulgence by my teacher.

Tiring

making you feel tired

  • I've had a very tiring day.
  • Taking care of the kids is extremely tiring.
  • They don't come down to London much because it's too tiring with the kids.
  • She's a lovely woman, even if she can be a bit tiring at times.
  • Looking after small children can be very tiring.

Worrying

making you feel unhappy and frightened

  • It's a very worrying situation.
  • A worrying trend for insurers has been a rise in fraudulent claims.
  • That's the most worrying aspect of the situation.
  • By midweek, the situation had become worrying.
  • He demonstrated a worrying naivety about political issues.
  • I heard something rather worrying at work this morning.

Following

going or proceeding or coming after in the same direction; about to be mentioned or specified

  • The weeks following the riots were extremely tense.
  • Following the dinner, there will be a dance.
  • Oil prices are certain to rise following the agreement to limit production.
  • Major changes are expected in the England team following their 3-0 defeat last Saturday.

Aging

growing old

  • Oil companies are particularly concerned about their aging fleet of deep-water rigs.
  • They are also aging in new oak barrels, giving their tequila a surprising aroma and taste of bourbon.
  • She thought of him as an aging hippy.

Dying

very ill and likely to die soon

  • She nursed her dying husband for months.
  • The patient nurse ministered to the dying old man.
  • The priest prayed for the dying man.
  • Refugees are dying for want of proper health care.

Working

relating to work

  • The union wants to see a reduction in working hours.
  • Insistence on better working conditions by the union has resulted in fewer employee absences.
  • The introduction of new working practices has dramatically improved productivity.
  • These figures mean that almost 15 percent of the working population is unemployed.

Living

pertaining to living persons; true to life; lifelike

  • Don't all living creatures have certain rights?
  • Water is essential for living things.
  • All living things are interdependent.
  • She would never harm another living creature.
  • They are hoping to discover living organisms in the soil.

Acting

someone who does a job for a short time while the person who usually does that job is not there

  • He'll be the acting director until they can appoint a permanent one.
  • Members of the group elected her to serve as acting chair.
  • He had previously been the acting assistant secretary for secondary education.

Paying

to give money to someone for work that they have done.

  • The company pays its interns $4,000 a month.
  • We pay €200 a day for this kind of work.
  • Accountancy may be boring but at least it pays well.
  • Most of these women are very poorly paid and work in terrible conditions.

Raging

very severe or extreme

  • I was in a raging temper.
  • Within minutes the house had become a raging inferno.
  • After heavy rain, the little stream becomes a raging torrent.
  • A blizzard was raging but the boy and girl were all bundled up.

Joking

something, such as a funny story or trick, that is said or done in order to make people laugh

  • I'm not joking. Those were his actual words.
  • We were strolling along, laughing and joking.
  • Oh, lighten up! I was only joking!

Caring

A caring person is kind and gives emotional support to others

  • I've always thought of Jo as a very caring person.
  • The government is deliberately trying to project a more caring image.
  • She was a very loving wife and mother and a caring friend.
  • He's very efficient and caring - I think he would make a good nurse.

Losing

suffering, resulting in, or relating to defeat in a game or contest.

  • He moaned with pain before losing consciousness.
  • He was mad at me for losing his watch.
  • I want someone afraid of losing me.
  • He started drinking to excess after losing his job.
  • She feared losing her inheritance to her stepmother.

Giving

providing love or other emotional support; caring.

  • True love is giving, not taking.
  • The art of giving presents is to give something which others cannot buy for themselves.
  • The radio is giving out a strange signal.
  • Giving up smoking reduces the risk of heart disease.

Coming

likely to be important or successful in the future.; due to something happening or just beginning.

  • Wish you happiness and prosperity in the coming year!
  • She was hesitant about coming forward with her story.
  • She didn't even call to say she wasn't coming.

Biting

used to describe weather that is extremely cold, especially when it causes you physical pain:

  • A cold, biting wind blew in from the north.
  • He made some biting remarks about the whole occasion.
  • A biting wind blew down from the hills.

Baking

very hot

  • It's really baking out there.
  • They worked all day under a baking hot sun.
  • It was baking hot inside the tents.
  • The flagstones were baking under her bare feet.
  • In the summer, most people escape the baking hot city for the coast or the mountains.

Roving

traveling from place to place

  • A roving band of performers will tour schools around the country to boost students' interest in verse. A roving camera crew interviewed participants and spectators.
  • He became known as a roving diplomat traveling to trouble spots across the globe.
  • His book describes all the horrors he has seen as a roving correspondent in Africa's war zones.
