PERFECT INFINITIVE AFTER VERBS

We form the perfect infinitive with to have + the -ed form of a verb. 

We use the perfect infinitive after verbs such as claim, expect, hate, hope, like, love, prefer, pretend:

  • He pretended to have lost her number and so had been unable to contact her. (or He pretended that he had lost her number …)

The perfect infinitive often refers to things that might have happened in the past:

  • She claims to have met a number of famous people, but I don’t believe her. (or She claims she has met …)

  • I would prefer to have stayed at a small, family-run hotel than a big international chain. (I prefer to stay at small hotels, but I did not.)

The perfect infinitive can refer to something that will be completed at a point in the future:

  • We hope to have finished the building works by the end of March.

The perfect infinitive is often used after the modal auxiliary verbs could, would, might, ought, should and needn’t to talk about unreal situations.

We can use the perfect infinitive in a clause with a verb that has no subject (a non-finite clause). It can refer to events which did happen in the past or to events that might have happened (but did not happen):

  • To have got the job in the face of such stiff competition was a great achievement. (The person did get the job.)

  • To have won the race would have been fantastic, but even coming second was a great achievement. (The person did not win the race.)

MORE SAMPLE SENTENCES:

  • I happened to have seen that film before.

  • I’d like to have met him.

  • He pretended not to have noticed me.

  • I got really wet walking home last night, I should have taken an umbrella.

  • It's better to have loved and lost than never to have loved at all. 

  • I am glad to have found a new job. 

  • She was sorry to have missed the concert.

  • You seem to have annoyed him.

  • You should have asked my permission before going out. 

  • She should have discussed the matter with him. 

  • I would have gone to university, if I had passed my exam. 

  • You shouldn’t have provoked him. 

  • We needn’t have waited for his approval. 

  • She should have arrived by now.

  • I am glad to have contacted you. 

  • We are delighted to have signed the papers.

  • I did not regret to have lived with them.

  • If you had called me, I could have helped you.

  • A Queensland tiger is known to have existed.

  • To have spoken at the convention, I would have needed an invitation.

  • To have gone by train, I would have needed a reservation.

  • To have thrown the ball, I would have needed a catcher.

  • To have known about the book, I would have had to see the ad.

  • To have eaten such a big dinner, I would have had to go without food for a day.

  • To have written the letter would have meant I would have had to own a typewriter.

  • To have chosen that coat, I would have had to have spent a fortune.

  • I am glad to have found a good job.

  • She was sorry to have missed the first prize.

  • We hope to have finished the job by next Sunday.

  • You seem to have annoyed him yesterday.

  • I meant to have sent the letter, but I forgot.

  • You should have called. I was getting worried.

  • I needn’t have watered the plants.

  • You shouldn’t have said those words to grandma.
