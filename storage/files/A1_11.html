Verb TO BE (affirmative, negative and interrogative)

The verb to be is used to say something about a person, thing, or state, to show a permanent or temporary quality, state, job or occupation, and nationality.

Verbs to Be in English come in three main categories: the affirmative, the negative, and the interrogative.

The affirmative - Asserting that something is true or valid.

EXAMPLES:

They have a car.

He's reading now.

They saw a movie.

We have been there.

I am American.

He is a teacher.

She is happy.

They are at the movies.

I was hungry.

She was at the movies.

You were there.

It was great!.

They were excited.

I am excited.

It is boring.

She is the leader.

They are at the bank.

The interrogative - Asking a question. he interrogative is formed by putting the verb before the subject.

EXAMPLES:

Is he happy?

Are they American?

Do they have a car?

Was it snowing?

Was I there?

Were you sad?

Was she at school?

Was it a good movie?

Were they late?

Are you the doctor?

Is she the teacher?

Is it cold outside?

Are you feeling better?

The negative - A reply denying something. The negative is formed by putting the not after the verb.

EXAMPLES:

I am not at the club.

We are not French.

She isn't hungry.

You aren't late.

I am not a student.

He is not a lawyer.

We are not doctors.

Stephen is not married.
