Crime and the Law

Assault:

To hit/injure someone physically.

  • He was guilty of assault and battery.
  • The rebels are poised for a new assault on the government garrisons.
  • The organizers of the march were charged with assault and riotous assembly.
  • At the police station, I was charged with assault.
  • Assault carries a maximum penalty of seven years' imprisonment.
  • They took part in a vigorous army assault course.

Blackmail:

To threaten to reveal incriminating materials if someone doesn't do something.

  • I'm never prepared to surrender to blackmail.
  • She had already tried emotional blackmail to stop him leaving.
  • She started off by accusing him of blackmail but he more or less ignored her.
  • The journalist used blackmail to make the lawyer give him the documents.

Burglary:

To steal or break into a home or car, etc.

  • Cash was taken during a burglary of the apartment.
  • We had new locks fitted after the burglary.
  • An 11-year-old boy committed a burglary.
  • Crime is on the increase, especially mugging and burglary.
  • I am placing you under arrest for attempted burglary.

Fraud:

A deception intended to result in financial or personal gain.

  • Fraud is an entirely different matter.
  • The story is about theft, fraud and deceit on an incredible scale.
  • This latest episode in the fraud scandal has shocked a lot of people.
  • He is fighting extradition to Hong Kong to face trial on fraud charges.

Hijacking:

Unlawfully seizing an aircraft, vehicle, or ship while in transit.

  • The hijacking became a trigger point for military action.
  • He made a desperate attempt to hijack a plane.
  • There have been a series of hijackings recently in the area.
  • He tried to hijack the guard into handing him the keys of the safe.

Hooliganism:

Violent or rowdy behavior that occurs (typically) in crowds or gangs.

  • The problem of hooliganism should be kept in proportion. Only a small number of young people act in this way.
  • The propensity to hooliganism is inherent in every society.
  • Hooliganism and segregation were mutually reinforcing.
  • He committed hooliganism even under the watchful eyes of the people.

Kidnapping:

The act of abducting someone and holding them captive.

  • The kidnapping occurred in broad daylight.
  • The kidnapping caused an international incident.
  • He faces charges of kidnapping and extortion.
  • He is charged with kidnapping a businessman last year and holding him for ransom.
  • The attack was in reprisal for the kidnapping of their leaders.

Mugging:

The act of attacking and robbing someone in a public place.

  • Crime is on the increase, especially mugging and burglary.
  • Police are concerned that mugging is on the increase.
  • Mugging is a real worry for many old people.
  • After that mugging, the relievers did much better work, especially during the last two games against the Yankees.

Appeal:

Asking for a reversal of the decision of a court.

  • The aid appeal has galvanized the German business community.
  • We were gratified by the response to our appeal.
  • The appeal was rejected by the High Court.
  • His appeal for forgiveness went unanswered.

Barrister:

A British term for a lawyer.

  • They chose a famous barrister to represent them in court.
  • She practiced as a barrister for many years.
  • There were on each circuit panels of barristers prepared to advise and act on behalf of any barrister threatened with a wasted costs order.
  • There are two exceptions: one case involving a barrister and an actress received widespread national coverage and were named.

Caution:

Care taken to avoid danger or mistakes.

  • In prosperity caution, in adversity patience.
  • You should exercise extreme caution when driving in fog.
  • The defendant may make a statement under caution.

Community service:

Voluntary work intended to help people in a particular area.

  • A well run community service can deal effectively with most skin problems.
  • Each was ordered to do 120 hours' community service and pay ￡850 costs.
  • A misleading assumption is that only less will be drawn into community service.
  • He was banned from driving for 6 months and ordered to do 200 hours of community service.

Court:

Place where cases and legal matters are conducted.

  • Are you prepared to repeat these allegations in court?
  • He lodged an appeal with the High Court.
  • Her lawyer made a statement outside the court.

Court case:

A dispute between two parties that is decided in a court of law.

  • The court case will do serious harm to my business.
  • The lawyer requested a break in the court case, but the judge demurred.
  • After the court case Lee was hounded relentlessly by the press.

Death penalty:

The punishment of execution.

  • The prosecutor bayed for a death penalty.
  • One of those arrested could face the death penalty.
  • The Supreme Court reinstated the death penalty in 1976.
  • Three Britons are facing the death penalty for spying.

Defense:

The case presented by or on behalf of the party being accused.

  • The Defense Secretary gave an upbeat assessment of the war so far.
  • The defense wants the district Judge to throw out the case.
  • Shea's testimony threw the defense off stride.
  • The defense attorney phrased his summation at last.

Fine:

The payment of money for being caught.

  • The maximum penalty for the offense is a $1,000 fine.
  • If found guilty, he faces six months in jail and a heavy fine.
  • I've just had to pay a $10 parking fine.
  • He did not have to pay his speeding fine because he pleaded diplomatic immunity.
  • They're appealing to the High Court to reduce the prison sentence to a fine.

Imprisonment:

The state of being imprisoned.

  • The death sentence was commuted to life imprisonment.
  • She was sentenced to five years imprisonment for manslaughter.
  • Imprisonment without trial is totally unacceptable in a democracy.
  • The Act states that anyone committing the offense is liable to imprisonment.

Innocent: 

Not being guilty of a crime.

  • She was found innocent of any crime.
  • She continued to assert that she was innocent.
  • In this country, you are innocent until proven guilty.
  • Many innocent civilians were killed during the war.

Judge:

An official appointed to decide cases in a court of law.

  • The judge reminded the witness that she was under oath.
  • The judge will pronounce sentence on the defendant this afternoon.
  • In Britain, judges wear white wigs in court.
  • She wrote an article attacking the judges and their conduct of the trial.
  • He shouted abuse at the judge after being sentenced to five years imprisonment.

Jury:

A group of people (typically twelve in number) sworn to give a verdict in a legal case on the basis of evidence submitted in court.

  • The jury delivered a verdict of not guilty.
  • The members of the jury left the court with a police escort.
  • Not surprisingly, the jury found them both guilty.
  • The decision about whether he is innocent or guilty rests with the jury.
  • He went free because the jury decided there was a reasonable doubt about his guilt.

Justice:

A judge or magistrate, or, the quality of fairness.

  • There's no justice in the world when people can be made to suffer like that.
  • The winner has been disqualified for cheating, so justice has been done.
  • he police are doing all they can to bring those responsible for the bombing to justice.
  • They are victims of a miscarriage of justice

Lawyer:

Someone who practices or studies law.

  • The lawyer proved the innocence of his client.
  • Her lawyer made a statement outside the court.
  •  Discussions between a lawyer and client are privileged communications.

Offense: 

A breach of law/illegal act.

  • The Redskins' offense is stronger than their defense.
  • I avoided giving offense by euphemizing.
  • Harboring criminals is an offense in law.
