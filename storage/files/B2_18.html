Phrasal verbs

A phrasal verb is a combination of a verb and an adverb or preposition, which has a particular meaning.

Phrasal verbs are two or more words that together act as a completely new word, with a meaning separate from the original words.

Let us take these sentences as examples for comparison:

  • We have to get the documents.
  • Men easily get over certain illnesses.

The original verb “get” means to obtain something. However, when added with a particle its meaning changes. In this case, the phrasal verb “get over” means to recover from an illness.

Phrasal verbs can be transitive or intransitive:

  • Transitive phrasal verbs are followed by a direct object.
  • Intransitive phrasal verbs are not followed by a direct object but can optionally be followed by an indirect object.

Transitive Phrasal Verbs

Transitive Phrasal verbs require an object to complete their meaning

If someone says: “I’m looking for”

You would automatically think “Looking for what? Looking for whom?”

  • I am looking for my keys.

My keys are the object (that you are looking for). Now the sentence is clear.

We need to add an object to make the sentence complete.

More examples of transitive phrasal verbs in sentences:

  • He’s looking for his passport.
  • You should put on a jacket because it’s cold outside.
  • Can you turn off the light when you leave the room please?

Intransitive Phrasal Verbs

Intransitive phrasal verbs cannot have a direct object after them.

  • My car broke down on the way to work.

Broke down in the past tense of break down. Break down means to stop working.

You cannot “break down something”. Break down is an intransitive phrasal verb.

  • Can you sit down please?

You cannot “sit down something”.

Some more example sentences with intransitive phrasal verbs:

  • I grew up in New Zealand.
  • You are driving too fast. Can you slow down?
  • We should dress up for the party.
  • Nobody found out that I didn’t have an invitation.
  • I get up at 7 every morning.
  • What time do you think he is going to show up?

Phrasal Verbs that are Transitive and Intransitive

Some phrasal verbs can be both transitive and intransitive.

They can be transitive in one sentence and intransitive in another sentence.

You need to be careful. Sometimes the meaning of a phrasal verb changes depending on whether it is transitive or intransitive. Let’s look at the following example:

Take off (transitive) = to remove something

  • He took off his tie when he got home.

Take off (intransitive) = to leave the ground and begin to fly

  • The plane will take off in ten minutes.

*Phrasal verbs are used in the same way as regular verbs in a sentence. In addition, we often use them in spoken English and in an informal setting. Thus, it is best to avoid using these verbs in formal writing.

Phrasal Verbs List

Run over

Meaning: If a vehicle or its driver runs a person or animal over, it knocks them down or drives over them.

Examples:

  • You can always run him over and make it look like an accident.
  • He ran over a six-year-old child as he was driving back from a party.

Look back

Meaning: To think about something that happened in the past.

Examples:

  • When I look back I can see where we went wrong.
  • It wasn't such a bad experience when I look back on it.
  • When I look back, I'm filled with sadness.
  • He looked back on his time as president with pride.
  • One day we'll look back on this and laugh.

Pay back

Meaning: If you pay someone back for doing something unpleasant to you, you take your revenge on them or make them suffer for what they did.

Examples:

  • Those workers are due a total of approximately $500,000 in back pay, according to union officials.
  •  He made the fatal error of borrowing more than he could pay back.

Turn into

Meaning: (turn into someone/something) to change or develop into something different.

Examples:

  • Our holiday turned into a nightmare.
  • Rain in the morning will turn into snow during the afternoon..
  • We turned into the dead-end street.
  • He has turned into a very pleasant fellow.

Burn out

Meaning: To become sick or very tired from working too hard.

Examples:

  • If Olivia keeps working late every night, she will burn out.
  • They were burned out and had to live with relatives.
  • Doing one task for too long can cause you to burn out.

Come in

Meaning: Join or become involved in an enterprise.

Examples:

  • That's where Jack comes in.
  • We need expert advice, and that's where you come in.
  • We're still waiting for copies of the book to come in.

Fall down

Meaning: To be shown to be not true or not good enough; To fall to the ground.

Examples:

  • And that's where the theory falls down.
  • I fell down because the soil was slippery.
  • His argument fell down partly because there was no evidence to support it.

Get back

Meaning: To return, especially to your home

Examples:

  • What time did you get back last night?
  • We only got back from our trip yesterday.
  • It’s late, I ought to get back.
  • Dad always got back home in time for dinner.

Cheer up

Meaning: To become more cheerful; to make someone or something more cheerful.

Examples:

  • Oh, come on—cheer up!
  • Give Mary a call; she needs some cheering up.
  • Bright curtains can cheer up a dull room.

Find out

Meaning: To get some information about something or someone by asking, reading, etc.

Examples:

  • She'd been seeing him for a while, but didn't want her parents to find out.
  • I haven't found anything out about him yet.
  • The police are determined to find out who killed Louise.

Look out

Meaning: Used to warn someone to be careful, especially when there is danger.

Examples:

Look out! There's a car coming.

Please look out when you’re crossing streets.

Pass away

Meaning: To die. People say ‘pass away’ to avoid saying ‘die’.

Examples:

  • His mother passed away last year.
  • He passed away in his sleep at the age of eighty-four.
  • Our chairman passed away yesterday, and his funeral will be held next Friday.
  • Olivia told us she wanted her body to be cremated after she passed away.

Think back

Meaning: Recall a past event or time.

Examples:

  • I’ve been trying to think back to that last evening.
  • I keep thinking back to the day I arrived here.
  • It might help you to understand Julia if you think back to when you were her age.

Break down

Meaning: stop functioning (vehicle, machine)

Examples:

  • Our car broke down at the side of the highway in the snowstorm.
  • Did his truck break down or something?

Break down

Meaning: get upset

Examples:

  • The woman broke down when the police told her that her son had died.
  • Because he was being so kind and concerned, I broke down and cried.

Bring someone down

Meaning: cause someone to lose power // make someone unhappy or depressed.

Examples:

  • Civil War in England eventually brought down the King.
  • She was in such a good mood I couldn't bear to bring her down.

Ask around

Meaning: ask many people the same question

Examples:

  • I asked around but nobody has seen my wallet.
  • We are going to ask around, to see if there is a house we can rent.

Break out

Meaning: (of war, fighting, or similarly undesirable things) start suddenly. / escape

Examples:

  • Apparently, this war seems to break out because of people.
  • The prisoners broke out of jail when the guards weren't looking.
  • Only one of them has the energy to break out.

Calm down

Meaning: relax after being angry

Examples:

  • You are still mad. You need to calm down before you drive the car.
  • Look, calm down! We'll find her.

Check out

Meaning: leave a hotel

Examples:

  • Guests should check out of their rooms by noon.
  • I'm sorry, they aren't here. They checked out this morning.

Come across something

Meaning: find unexpectedly

  • Examples:
  • I came across these old photos when I was tidying the closet.
  • Why don't you come across to our house this evening?

Work out

Meaning: exercise // be successful

Examples:

  • I work out at the gym three times a week.
  • Our plan worked out fine.

Warm up

Meaning: prepare body for exercise

Examples:

  • I always warm up by doing sit-ups before I go for a run.
  • Aim to do the exercises after a proper warm up and stretch.

Wake up

Meaning: stop sleeping

Examples:

  • We have to wake up early for work on Monday.
  • The sound of a door slamming woke him up.

Turn something off

Meaning: stop the energy flow, switch off

Examples:

  • Your mother wants you to turn the TV off and come for dinner.
  • Teaching off a blackboard is boring, and undoubtedly turns people off.

Turn something on

Meaning: start the energy, switch on

Examples:

  • It's too dark in here. Let's turn some lights on.
  • I want to turn on the television.

Turn something up

Meaning: increase the volume or strength (heat, light etc)

Examples:

  • Can you turn the music up? This is my favourite song.
  • Turn the heat up high.

Think back

Meaning: remember (often + to, sometimes + on)

Examples:

  • When I think back on my youth, I wish I had studied harder.
  • I thought back to the time in 1975 when my son was desperately ill.

Think something over

Meaning: consider

Examples:

  • I'll have to think this job offer over before I make my final decision.
  • I suggest you think over your position very carefully.

Take off

Meaning: start to fly

Examples:

  • My plane takes off in five minutes.
  • We eventually took off at 11 o'clock and arrived in Venice at 1.30

Switch something off

Meaning: stop the energy flow, turn off

Examples:

  • The light's too bright. Could you switch it off?
  • She switched off the coffee-machine.

Switch something on

Meaning: start the energy flow, turn on

Examples:

  • We heard the news as soon as we switched on the car radio.
  • She emptied both their mugs and switched on the electric kettle.
