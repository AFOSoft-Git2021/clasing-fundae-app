EXPRESSING EMPHASIS: ADDING WORDS FOR EMPHASIS

1. Adding “own” to intensify possessive adjectives: 

  • It was my own idea, and nobody else’s!

 2. Adding “very” and “indeed”: 

a) “Very” can be used in an emphatic manner to mean “exactly” or “precisely”: 

  • ... and that got me to thinking... How many million people around the world were doing that very same thing at that very same moment? 

b) “Very ... indeed” can be used to intensify adjectives: 

  • I don’t like adverts in general, but there are some that are very, very stupid and really get me irritated.

3. Emphasizing negatives: 

a) To emphasize “not”: at all, in the least, really, the least bit. 

  • Shayla is not the least bit attractive —in my opinion, of course.

  • Her reaction doesn’t surprise me in the slightest . 

b) To emphasize “no” and “none”: at all, whatsoever. 

  • He took deep breaths... ragged breaths... and the tears fell until there were none left whatsoever . 

4. Adding “the” to emphasize uniqueness. It is heavily stressed in speech. 

  • Surely you are not THE Paul Newman, are you? 

5. Adding question words that end in “-ever” to add an air of disbelief to the question. 

  • I wanna know whoever told you I was watching that sort of program? 

6. Adding auxiliary “do” to emphasize the verb. It is stressed in speech. 

I do like Sarah... It’s only that sometimes she can be a little too pushy.

7. Adding adverbs and adjectives:

  • I actually visited Elvis Presley’s Graceland. 

  • This is the sheer truth, I promise!

  • Small families breed pampered children? What utter nonsense! 

With non-gradable adjectives (adjectives that express an absolute opinion):

  • I recommend it. We had an absolutely fantastic time there. 

I must confess I found her advice utterly useless. e.g.: Her dressing style is simplydelightful.
