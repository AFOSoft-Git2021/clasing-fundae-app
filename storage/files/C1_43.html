Business Idioms & Slang (Part 2)

Big fish

MEANING: An important or influential person.

SENTENCES:

  • Andrew became a big fish in the world of politics.

  • Until then only minions had been arrested, but now they turned their attention to the big fish.

  • The four men arrested were described as really big fish by the U.S. Drug Enforcement Agency.

Bury your head in the sand

MEANING: To ignore a situation

SENTENCES:

  • You have to face facts here - you can't just bury your head in the sand.

  • If you bury your head in the sand now, you may lose your house.

  • You've got to face facts here - you can't just bury your head in the sand.

Smell a rat

MEANING: To sense something is wrong

SENTENCES:

  • He's been working late with her every night this week - I smell a rat!

  • When I got an email asking for my password, I should have smelled a rat.

  • The investor doesn't start to smell a rat until the payments aren't coming in.

  • I don't want to say he steals them, but I really smell a rat.

Under someone's thumb

MEANING:  To be controlled by someone else

SENTENCES:

  • He has the committee firmly under his thumb - they agree to whatever he asks.

  • You can't keep your kids under your thumb all their lives. I don't want to have these people under my thumb. I'm not the manager type.

  • Now that they’re married, she’s completely under his thumb and never sees her old friends.

  • You can’t keep your kids under your thumb all their lives.

Curiosity killed the cat

MEANING: To be curious and get into trouble

SENTENCES:

  • Please stop prying into my private life. Remember, curiosity killed the cat.

  • When he started asking too many questions of his neighbors about their whereabouts during the weekend, they warned him that curiosity killed the cat.

  • When Jane asked George where he was going in the middle of the night, he replied that curiosity killed the cat.

  • Joe was very curious about where Sarah was getting all her money from, but all she said was that curiosity killed the cat.

The powers that be

MEANING: The people in control

SENTENCES:

  • The students wanted to have a big party, but the powers that be didn't approve.

  • It's up to the powers that be to decide what should be done next.

Copycat

MEANING: To do the same as someone else

SENTENCES:

  • At the market you can buy affordable copycat versions of expensive perfumes.

  • Sales of the product have fallen by 42% since copycat versions were launched.

  • The jeans company says it will fight to have all copycats removed from stores and destroyed.

  • High-end products were therefore insulated from the encroachment of cheap copycats.

Smell something fishy

MEANING: To be suspicious

SENTENCES:

  • The manager smelled something fishy and started an investigation. He eventually found that 3 of the 4 employees were stealing from the store. 

  • There’s something fishy with this report card, son. It looks like you altered it with white-out to change your grade.

  • Something smells fishy with this situation. The backup was supposed to be here five minutes ago, and all the radios just went dead.

Turn a blind eye

MEANING: To not take notice of something 

SENTENCES:

  • Management often turns a blind eye to bullying in the workplace.

  • Many landlords turn a blind eye to the fact that two families are sharing apartments.

  • Whenever we turn a blind eye to injustice, we embrace separation instead of oneness.

  • Don't turn a blind eye to matters that concern the people's welfare.

Pull a few strings

MEANING: To use your influence to your own advantage

SENTENCES:

  • I may be able to pull a few strings if you need the document urgently.

  • I hear that the only reason Johnny got into college was because his dad pulled some strings with the president of the university.

  • I actually know a few people who work at the restaurant, so I'll see if I can pull a few strings and get you a table.

  • You wait and see, he's going to start pulling strings to get this police investigation quashed.

Gain the upper hand

MEANING: Take advantage of something

SENTENCES:

  • The government was beginning to gain the upper hand.

  • Officials said they might reopen the freeway at 5 p. m. today, perhaps sooner if firefighters gain the upper hand overnight.

  • Realizing that he was the better fighter, I decided to gain the upper hand by launching a surprise attack.

Give someone a hand

MEANING: To help with something

SENTENCES:

  • Marcia has taught her children to lend a hand when it comes to cleaning up after meals.

  • We promised to lend a hand at church today by calling parishioners for donations.

  • Your grandson is so kind. He always lends me a hand with repairs around the house whenever he's in town.

Keep an eye on something

MEANING: To observe something

SENTENCES:

  • Keep an eye on your brother while I’m out, please.

  • Please keep an eye on my bag while I go to the bathroom.

  • The police kept an eye on the drunk people at the carnival.

  • We have to keep an eye on what our competitors are doing.

Have a catnap

MEANING: To sleep for a short time

SENTENCES:

  • I had a very late night last night. I think that I will have a catnap during my lunch hour. Since I am on vacation I take a catnap every day.

  • He had cultivated the ability to catnap at odd moments during a day.

Give the green light

MEANING: To give permission

SENTENCES:

  • The government has decided to give the green light to the plan.

  • The council has given the green light to the new shopping center.

  • His boss finally gave him the green light to start the new project.

Be a busybody

MEANING: To be nosy/to gossip about something

SENTENCES:

  • He's an interfering old busybody - who I go out with is none of his business!

  • I can assure you that any busybody would be hard put to it to prove maltreatment!

  • When she was a child she had regarded her mother as an interfering busybody.

Know it all

MEANING: To think you know anything

SENTENCES:

  • You never want to come across as a know-it-all, no matter what age you are.

  • People in general can tell when someone truly knows what they're talking about and when they are just trying to be a know-it-all.

  • Today, new employees arrive on their first day with an alarming amount of know-it-all.

Against the clock

MEANING: Rushed and short on time

SENTENCES:

  • I was racing against the clock, but I finished the test just before the bell.

  • We are working against the clock to finish the report by Friday.

  • The emergency services were working against the clock as the tide began to rise.

  • The emergency services were working against the clock as the tide began to rise.

  • We'll have to work against the clock if we want to launch the website on time.

All In The Same Boat

MEANING: When everyone is facing the same challenges

SENTENCES:

  • She's always complaining that she doesn't have enough money, but we're all in the same boat.

  • It's no use quarreling - we're all in the same boat.

  • Everyone has lost their job. We're all in the same boat.

At the drop of a hat

MEANING: Willing to do something immediately

SENTENCES:

  • People will file lawsuits at the drop of a hat these days.

  • I hate to speak in public, but she’ll get up on stage at the drop of a hat.

  • The boy is quarrelsome and ready to fight at the drop of a hat.

  • Some of these corporations threaten to sue at the drop of a hat.

Graveyard Shift

MEANING: Working hours from about 12:00 am to 8:00 am

SENTENCES:

  • He paid for his studies by working the graveyard shift as a hospital cleaner.

  • I've been really tired since I started working the graveyard shift.

  • He suggested we should go to eat after my graveyard shift.

  • His wife is not happy with her husband's decision to take a graveyard shift.

Gut Feeling

MEANING: A personal intuition you get, especially when feel something may not be right

SENTENCES:

  • He had a gut feeling that Sarah was lying.

  • My gut feeling was that we couldn't trust her.

  • I had a gut feeling Eric was the right man for us and good value at the price.

  • We worked on gut feeling and it was very difficult to control and manage all the development work because of the technology involved.

Hit The Nail on the Head

MEANING: Do something exactly right or say something exactly right

SENTENCES:

  • I think Mick hit the nail on the head when he said that what's lacking in this company is a feeling of confidence.

  • I think David hit the nail on the head when he said that kids won't want to buy this product.

  • He hit the nail on the head, because management is nothing more than motivating other people.

Get something off the ground

MEANING: To start something (e.g. a project or a business)

SENTENCES:

  • A lot more money will be required to get this project off the ground.

  • He and his friend tried to start a band but it never got off the ground.

  • If you have to ask others for help to get something off the ground, don't forget to thank them afterwards.

  • It is not so much about genuinely getting something off the ground now.

Long shot

MEANING: Something that has a very low probability of happening

SENTENCES:

  • It's a long shot, but you could try phoning him at home.

  • It's a long shot, but someone might recognise her from the photo and be able to tell us where she lives.

  • She had looked everywhere else and, although it seemed a long shot, she might as well look in there.

Put all your eggs in one basket

MEANING: To rely on only one thing to bring success

SENTENCES:

  • I'm applying for several jobs because I don't really want to put all my eggs in one basket.

  • I put all my eggs in one basket when I quit my job and moved out west—fortunately, I found an apartment and a job before I spent all of my savings.

  • Athletes often put all of their eggs in one basket and when they get seriously injured their careers and lives are ruined.

  • I was so sure that I'd get that job that I turned down two other employment offers. Of course my parents warned me not to put all of my eggs in one basket. 

  • I really want to be an actor but I've started writing screenplays and have also learned how to edit because I don't want to put all of my eggs in one basket.

Rock the boat

MEANING: To do or say something that will upset people or cause problems

SENTENCES:

  • Don't rock the boat until the negotiations are finished.

  • It's better not to rock the boat when you first start a job.

Take the bull by the horns

MEANING: To directly confront a difficult situation in a brave and determined way

SENTENCES:

  • I took the bull by the horns and confronted him about his mistreatment of the workers.

  • Nora decided to take the bull by the horns and organize things for herself.

  • It's time to take the bull by the horns. I'm going to demand reform in the sales department even though it may mean risking my job.

  • He decided to take the bull by the horns and demand a raise even though he might get fired.

The elephant in the room

MEANING: An obvious problem or controversial issue that no one wants to discuss.

SENTENCES:

  • I don't want to ask the question, but it is the big elephant in the room.

  • The anchor responded that the caller had pointed out the elephant in the room that, until then, the media was hesitant to suggest.

  • It was the elephant in the room they simply chose to ignore.

Hands are tied

MEANING: Not being free to behave in the way that you would like

SENTENCES:

  • I'd like to raise people's salaries but my hands are tied.

  • The company's hands are tied because of government regulations.

  • Politicians are always saying that they want to help us but their hands are tied.
