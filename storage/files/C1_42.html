Business Idioms & Slang (Part 1)

In no time

MEANING: Very quickly

SENTENCES:

  • Now that we’re on the highway, we’ll be there in no time.

  • We zipped through customs in no time.

  • Clothing seems to wear out in no time nowadays.

  • The whole building was dimmed out in no time.

  • She'll have them eating out of her hand in no time.

  • A couple of dozen prints were rolled off in no time.

No-brainer

MEANING: Something such as a decision that is very easy or obvious

SENTENCES:

  • The decision was a complete no-brainer.

  • Taking that job over the one I had was a no-brainer.

  • The decision to invest in the energy company was a no-brainer.

  • That last test question was a complete no-brainer.

Screw up

MEANING: To make a big mistake or blunder; to mishandle or ruin something

SENTENCES:

  • I totally screwed up the chemistry test.

  • Just don't screw up this time.

  • It really screwed him up when he saw his friend get killed.

  • There's been a screw-up with our hotel reservation.

  • You couldn’t screw up much worse than I did.

No biggie

MEANING: to not be a serious problem

SENTENCES:

  • Because the sunglasses are cheap, losing a pair is no biggie.

  • I was a little disappointed that they don't serve brunch on Saturdays, but hey, no biggie.

Wrap up

MEANING: To summarize or recapitulate

SENTENCES:

  • It’s getting late – let’s wrap it up.

  • She wrapped up a deal just before she left on vacation.

  • My guess is they'll wrap the meeting up fairly quickly.

  • All right, Chris, we're going to have to wrap it up there.

Circle back

MEANING: Return to a previous point or issue at a later time

SENTENCES:

  • He decided to walk and circle back, strolling like it was in the May sunshine.

  • In a way, I've almost come full circle back to teaching.

Game changer

MEANING: An event, idea, or procedure that effects a significant shift in the current way of doing or thinking about something.

SENTENCES:

  • As a player he can be a game changer.

  • The hit show has been a game changer for the network.

  • This is a historical game changer never before realized in the history of humankind.

  • I really believe this report is a game changer.

Boil the ocean

MEANING: An action or project that wastes a lot of time

SENTENCES:

  • Your suggestion that we solve all of these problems at one time is boiling the ocean.

Out of pocket

MEANING: Unavailable or out of the office for a disclosed period of time

SENTENCES:

  • All out-of-pocket expenses will be reimbursed by the company.

  • Under the new program, there are no out-of-pocket costs for preventative medical care.

  • There would be a week or two when he would be out of pocket.

  • Statements with errors could still be going out, but customers who notify us will not be left out of pocket.

Key takeaways

MEANING: are major points or areas of interest that are important to remember from a presentation or meeting.

SENTENCES:

  • The key takeaway is that a small code size and open source distribution are desirable to prove a system to be reliable and secure.

  • To get the ball rolling, we've rounded up five key takeaways from the debate.

  • So instead of a tidy bow, here are eight key takeaways from the series.

Bring to the table/party

MEANING: Used to describe a certain skill or expertise that an individual can offer to a company or project

SENTENCES:

  • He brings years of leadership experience to the table.

  • When you're writing your résumé, you need to make sure you highlight what great things you'll be bringing to the table.

Reinvent the wheel

MEANING: Create something that already exists

SENTENCES:

  • We don't need to reinvent the wheel, we just need to hire someone who already knows how to make the system work.

  • Without mentors we have to reinvent the wheel for each new generation.

  • Some of these ideas are worth pursuing, but there is no need to reinvent the wheel.

Ducks in a row

MEANING: To organize your tasks and schedule so that you are ready for the next step

SENTENCES:

  • They should have had their ducks in a row beforehand, so they were ready to start the job when required.

  • I wish we could join you but we have to get our ducks in a row before our move this weekend. 

  • Too bad you didn't have your ducks in a row before you quit your job.

Roll up your sleeves

MEANING: To prepare for hard work

SENTENCES:

  • There's a lot of work to do, so roll up your sleeves and get busy.

  • We've planned everything – now it's time to roll up our sleeves and get started.

  • If you want to get something done, just roll up your sleeves and do it.

Add fuel to the fire

MEANING: To make a bad situation even worse than it is

SENTENCES:

  • The discovery that the government was aware of the cover-up has really added fuel to the fire.

  • The President said he wouldn’t add fuel to the fire by commenting without knowing all the facts.

  • I was late and, to add insult to injury, forgot my keys.

Beat around the bush

MEANING: To make a bad situation even worse than it is

SENTENCES:

  • Don't beat around the bush - get to the point!

  • Quit beating around the bush and say what’s on your mind.

  • Let us be frank and don't beat around the bush.

  • He would not answer yes or no, but beat around the bush.

Bend over backwards

MEANING: Avoid the main topic or not speak directly about the issue

SENTENCES:

  • I've been bending over backwards trying to help you, and this is all the thanks I get!

  • City officials bent over backwards to help downtown businesses.

  • Mark will bend over backwards in order to please his girlfriend.

Cut corners

MEANING: Do something badly or cheaply

SENTENCES:

  • Take your time, don't cut corners and follow instructions to the letter.

  • There's always a temptation to cut corners when you're pushed for time, but it's not usually worth it.

  • Gas prices bring on new ways to cut corners.

  • Don't hurry. You cannot cut corners if you want to do a perfect job.

  • I want something deeper than the stuff you usually do for me, so don't look for ways to cut corners.

Win-win situation/result

MEANING:  situation in which each party benefits in some way

SENTENCES:

  • This is a win-win situation for her, because whoever wins this match, she's still going to be champion.

  • Flexible and shorter work weeks are a win-win situation for both the employee and employer.

  • Bringing law and order to the mean streets of Bronx is a win-win situation for the authority.

Rock bottom 

MEANING: the lowest possible level.

SENTENCES:

  • Prices have reached rock-bottom.

  • Confidence in the government is at rock bottom.

  • Prices have reached rock bottom.

  • They were selling the goods at rock-bottom prices.

  • The president's approval ratings have hit rock bottom.

Be on the same page

MEANING: In agreement

SENTENCES:

  • We're all on the same page here; we know what we're doing is incredibly valuable but we need some organization to better accomplish our objectives.

  • If you have a spouse, be sure to discuss every detail with him or her so you're both on the same page.

  • A good plan not only keeps you safe, but it also ensures that everyone in your group is on the same page.

Call it a day

MEANING: Decide or agree to stop doing something

SENTENCES:

  • I'm getting a little tired now - let's call it a day.

  • After 14 years living and working in this country, she thinks it's time to call it a day.

  • ve been studying hard – I think you should call it a day.

In a nutshell

MEANING: In the fewest possible words

SENTENCES:

  • In a nutshell, the owners thought they knew best.

  • Bob put it in a nutshell when he said the problem was essentially a lack of communication.

  • A study of women at work says, in a nutshell, that opportunities have opened up dramatically.

  • In a nutshell, the state government is expected to be $2 million in debt by the end of the year.

Go the extra mile

MEANING: To make more effort than is expected of you

SENTENCES:

  • He's a nice guy, always ready to go the extra mile for his friends.

  • The president expressed his determination to go the extra mile for peace.

  • He would go the extra mile to try to elucidate his intentions.

  • All this when her only motivation was to go the extra mile under all circumstances.

Shoot from the hip

MEANING: To speak up directly

SENTENCES:

  • Jeff is a straight talker who always shoots from the hip.

  • We're not going to shoot from the hip - we're going to think things through.

  • He often shoots from the hip without having all the facts.

  • Judges don't have to shoot from the hip. They have the leisure to think, to decide.

Kill two birds with one stone

MEANING: To achieve two things by doing a single action.

SENTENCES:

  • You can kill two birds with one stone by this method, which not only makes you tough, but also helps you learn to speak English smoothly.

  • Here you can not only make the food they like by hand, but also learn various English words of ingredients. Learn cooking and practice English, which is to kill two birds with one stone.

  • Every morning I take my son to school. And he enjoys listening to the English programmer on the way. One of my passengers once said my son is very clever to kill two birds with one stone.

Let the cat out of the bag

MEANING: Accidentally reveal a secret

SENTENCES:

  • I was trying to keep the party a secret, but Mel went and let the cat out of the bag.

  • I wanted my mother's present to be a secret, but my sister let the cat out of the bag.

  • I'm sorry. Jim knows about last week's party. I'm afraid I let the cat out of the bag.

Go down the drain

MEANING: Something is wasted or lost

SENTENCES:

  • All our efforts in entering this new market went down the drain.

  • Safety standards have gone down the drain.

  • There are fears of family life going down the drain, as staff may get only two complete weekends off in seven.

  • I can't believe it, four years go down the drain.

Bite off more than you can chew

MEANING: Take on a task or commitment that is too big

SENTENCES:

  • We bit off more than we could chew in our original reform proposals.

  • Don't be afraid to bite off more than you can chew.

No strings attached

MEANING: Something is given without involving special demands or limits

SENTENCES:

  • They will let you try the product for free with no strings attached.

  • Howard's agreed to lend me the money with no strings attached.

  • It's a business proposition, pure and simple. No strings attached.
