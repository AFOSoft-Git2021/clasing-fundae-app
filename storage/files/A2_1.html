Adverbs of Place

Adverbs that change or qualify the meaning of a sentence by telling us where things happen are defined as adverbs of place. Some instructors refer to these words or phrases as spatial adverbs. No matter what they are called, these adverbs always answer one important question: Where?

Adverb or preposition

Some adverbs of place also function as prepositions. For example, the adverb ‘down’ can be a preposition and an adverb. Its place in the sentence tells us if ‘down’ is functioning as a preposition or as an adverb. Remember that the adverb is placed after the verb and describes how it is done. Whereas the preposition is put before a noun and is not used to describe the verb.

Subject + main verb + adverb of place

  • e.g. She went inside.

Subject + main verb + preposition + noun (object)

  • e.g. She went inside the house.

Adverbs of place that are also prepositions include: around, behind, down, in, off, on, over, etc. These fall under the category of locational prepositions.

Anywhere, somewhere, elsewhere, everywhere

Adverb Example Meaning
Anywhere I can’t find it anywhere. Not in any place.
Somewhere I know I put it somewhere. In a place, but I don’t know where it is.
Elsewhere I’ll have to look elsewhere. In another place.
Everywhere I’ve looked everywhere. In all places.

Indoors, outdoors, inside, outside

This group of adverbs allow us to describe the location of something in relation to a building. When we say something is ‘indoors’, we mean it is in the house, whereas we use ‘outdoors’ to describe something or someone that is not in the building.

The adverbs ‘inside’ and ‘outside’ function in a similar way. These adverbs can also suggest movement, e.g. “I’m going outside” means I’m moving from being in a building to being out of a building.

Adverbials of place

We use adverbials to talk about where something is. An adverbial of place is a phrase that functions in the same way as an adverb of place. It gives more information about the verb. Example:

  • Becky was sitting in the garden.
  • She put the plate on the table.

Some common adverbs of place are used to form adverbial phrases. In these cases ‘there’ and ‘here’ become nouns.Example:

  • Over there.
  • Up here.

How do we use them?

1. Sentence position

a. We usually put adverbs of place at the end of a clause. They appear after the main verb. The position of the adverb does not change in a negative sentence.

  • Subject + main verb + adverb of place
  • The man walked backwards.
  • She didn’t walk away.

b. Adverbs of place can also occur after the object.

  • Subject + main verb + object + adverb of place
  • She didn’t find the book there.

- c. An adverb of place cannot be put between a verb and its direct object. So we cannot say: She didn’t find there the book.

2. Modal verbs

  • With modal verbs, the adverb of place appears after the main verb or after the object.
  • Subject + modal + main verb + adverb of place
  • e.g. You mustn’t run away.
  • Subject + modal + main verb + object + adverb of place
  • e.g. You should put that book there.

3. Literary uses

Adverbs of place are used more flexibly in literary writing (stories) than in everyday English.

This is to create drama and interest in the story. In this type of writing, the adverb can appear at the start of the sentence.

  • Adverb of place + Subject + main verb
  • Inside, all was quiet.
  • Nearby, a dog barked loudly.

4. Word order with more than one adverb

In a sentence with adverbs of time and place, the adverb of place appears before the adverb of time:

  • I’m moving abroad soon.
  • I’m taking the dog out this morning.

Here’ and ‘there’ - The adverbs ‘here’ and ‘there’ often appear at the start of a sentence.

  • Adverb of place + main verb + subject
  • Here comes the train!
  • There goes Tom.
Adverbs of place Meaning EXAMPLES
About used to indicate movement in an area. -The ball rolled about on the floor. - At the party, there were lots of kids running about.
Above in or to a higher position than something else - He put the book on the shelf above. - See the examples listed above.
Abroad (also Overseas) in or to a foreign country or countries -They have decided to move abroad. - Sue is planning to find a new job abroad.
Across from one side to the other of something with clear limits, such as an area of land, a road, or a river -They're building a new bridge across the river. - She walked across the road.
Ahead in front -You run ahead and we’ll catch you up. -If you want to win the race, you need to stay ahead.
Along from one part of a road, river, etc. to another - The brake was off so the car just rolled along. - Run along! It’s time for bed.
Anywhere (American English: Anyplace) in, to, or at any place: - I can’t find that book anywhere. - Come in! You can sit anywhere you like!
Around, Round in a position or direction surrounding, or in a direction going along the edge of or from one part to another - Will you children please stop running around?! - The moon goes round the earth.
Away somewhere else, or to or in a different place, position, or situation - Joe, it’s time to put all the toys away. - Do you remember the date she went away (on holiday)?
By used to show the person or thing that does something - John waited, but the beautiful woman just walked by. - I just drove by to say “hello”.
Below in a lower position (than), under: - Please see the examples below. - I think she lives in the flat below.
Down in or towards a low or lower position, from a higher one. Moving from above and onto a surface: - Please, sit down. - I enjoyed watching the sun go down.
Downstairs to or on a lower floor of a building - She heard the doorbell ring so she ran downstairs. - Be careful when you go downstairs.
East, West, North, South Known to be as cardinal directions that people around the world use - The man said he was travelling north. - We drove east via the A11.
Eastwards, Westwards, Northwards, Southwards towards a specific direction as North, East, etc. - We sailed northwards to the island. - We continued walking southwards towards the mountains.
Far used to indicate the extent to which one thing is distant from anothe - Have you walked far? - She told the children not to go far from home.
Here in, at, or to this place or position. -Where is Tony? Oh, here he comes! -I’m sure I left it here!
There in, at, or to that place or position. Put it in a place away from someone. - Put it there. -We went on to Paris and stayed there eleven days
Over in a position that is covering something: - He turned over and went back to sleep. - She held the umbrella over both of us.
Off used with actions in which something is removed or removes itself from another thin - Let's get off at the next stop. - Take your jacket off.
upwards towards a higher position, level, or value - She peered upward at the sky. - She turned her face upwards to the sun.
near not far away in distance - A bomb exploding somewhere near. -Is there a restaurant near here?
under in or to a position below or lower than something else, often so that one thing covers the other - The streams that ran under the melting glaciers. - He hid under the bed.
behind at the back (of) - I hung my coat behind the door. -After the third lap, she was way behind the other runners.
backwards towards the direction that is opposite to the one in which you are facing or opposite to the usual direction - I walked backwards towards the door. - He took a step backwards to allow her to pass.
nearby not far away: - I noticed a policeman standing nearby. - We stopped at some nearby shops to buy some food.
