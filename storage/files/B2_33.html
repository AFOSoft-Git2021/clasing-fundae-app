Useful Expressions

To enjoy

To get pleasure from something.

  • Take time to enjoy the simple things in life.
  • He welcomed you to enjoy his hospitality.
  • She wanted to enjoy her moment of glory.
  • They are entitled to enjoy many advantages and privileges.
  • His sole aim in life is to enjoy himself.

To avoid

To prevent something from happening or to not allow yourself to do something:

  • The submarine submerged to avoid enemy ships.
  • Book early for the show to avoid disappointment.
  • Bridges are used to avoid the intersection of a railway and a highway.
  • The President is eager to avoid a head-on clash with Congress over his health policies.

To hate

To strongly dislike someone or something

  • I used to hate being teased about my red hair when I was at school.
  • He came to hate the town, with its narrow prejudices.
  • When I was a child, I used to hate being kissed by visiting relatives.
  • It appears that the United States needs to have someone to blame, people to hate, a group to rally against.

To leap

To make a large jump or sudden movement, usually from one place to another; To achieve something suddenly, usually fame, power, or importance.

  • One name seemed to leap off the page from the list of candidates.
  • Balance existed to tilt off, floors to leap up from and air to fall or cantilever through.
  • In his haste to leap out of the way Vincent dropped and broke his paintbox.
  •  He seized the broomstick between his strong teeth and began to leap about, trying to wrest it from Angela's grasp.
  • The cat crouched, ready to leap.

To enable

To make someone able to do something, or to make something possible.

  • We need to record sufficient data to enable definite conclusions to be reached.
  • The King was on a private visit to enable him to pray at the tombs of his ancestors.
  • We will seek new arrangements to enable first-time buyers to concentrate relief in the early years.
  •  On the contrary, the aim was to enable them to give expression to their desires.

To assist

To take action to help someone or support something.

  • You will be employed to assist in the development of new equipment.
  • The public is urgently requested to assist police in tracing this man.
  • Migrating birds make use of airstreams to assist them on their long journey south.
  • I have taken this course solely to assist the realization of my assets for the benefit of all my creditors.

To cope with

To deal successfully with a difficult situation.

  • They have to cope with a mountain of problems.
  • She's struggling to cope with the heavy workload.
  • It must be really hard to cope with three young children and a job.
  • His indefatigable spirit helped him to cope with his illness.
  • Some people find unemployment very difficult to cope with.

To solve

To find an answer to a problem.

  • She tried her best to solve the problem.
  • Attempts are being made to solve the problem of waste disposal.
  • Human societies have the power to solve the problems confronting them.
  • The only way to solve homelessness is to provide more homes.
  • The detectives were beginning to solve the murder. All the pieces in the puzzle were falling neatly into place.

To breed 

To keep animals for the purpose of producing young animals in a controlled way.

  • Seals come ashore to breed.
  • Green turtles return to their natal island to breed.
  • The whales migrate some 6,000 miles to breed and calve in the warm lagoons.

To graze

(of animals) to eat grass, or to cause animals to feed on grass

  • The animals tend to graze in herds.
  • Villagers traditionally have the right to graze their cattle on the common land.
  • The land is now being used to graze cattle.
  • The camels were hobbled out to graze in the paddock.

To pay the price

To suffer the consequences of someone's actions.

  • Jack drank so much last night! Now he's paying the price for it
  • You have to pay the price but if you do you can only win.
  • The foreign minister had to pay the price for his disloyalty to the government and resign.
  • Happiness belongs to those who dream dreams and are ready to pay the price to make them come true.

To sew

To make a piece of clothing by joining pieces of cloth together by putting thread through them with a needle.

  • My mother taught me how to sew.
  • I'll have to sew a patch onto these jeans - they're ripped at the knee.
  • Surgeons have managed to sew a man's ear back on after it was bitten off in a fight.
  • She learned to sew and made maternity smocks he tried to admire.

To bargain

An agreement between two or more parties as to what each party will do for the other; A thing bought or offered for sale more cheaply than is usual or expected.

  • I refuse to bargain over the price.
  • The leaders refused to bargain away the freedom of their people.
  • They need hostages in order to bargain with the government.
  • He was prepared to bargain about money.
  • For a small sum they were content to bargain away their happiness.

To mean

To be intended to.

  • American people use "fall" to mean autumn.
  • Many people expected the growth of television to mean the eclipse of radio.
  • On the domestic front, disposal tends to mean throwing rubbish in the bin.

To encourage

To make someone more likely to do something, or to make something more likely to happen.

  • They'd got a special grant to encourage research.
  • The government wants to encourage viable self-contained rural communities.
  • We want to encourage good teamwork and communication.
  •  We need to encourage innovation in industry.
  • The company says the aim is to encourage teamwork.

To hunt

To chase and try to catch and kill an animal or bird for food, sport, or profit.

  • November is a good time to hunt deer.
  • The government agency was created to hunt down war criminals.
  • Today men across the land continue to hunt compulsively, despite a possibly declining need or appetite for the available game.
  • Bowman forced his fingers to hunt around, and presently discovered the pear-shaped bulb.

To drop

To fall or to allow something to fall.

  • The policeman ordered the criminal to drop his weapon.
  • As the pace quickened, Pepe began to drop behind.
  • Be careful not to drop that plate.
  • Students are allowed to drop history in Year 9.
  • Try not to drop into your native language.

To hang out with

Spend time relaxing or socializing informally.

  • I used to hang out with them when I was at college.
  • I am not going to hang out with him anymore because he is sleazy.
  • The thing is that a lot of people I like to hang out with think she is too weird.

To monitor

A person who has the job of watching or noticing particular things.

  • Officials had not been allowed to monitor the voting.
  • The police use electronic tags to monitor the whereabouts of young offenders on probation.
  • An independent body has been set up to monitor government spending.
  • Regular tests enable the teacher to monitor the progress of each child.

To make a fuss

Become angry and complain.

  • Beth is not the type to make a fuss.
  • U.S. imperialism is not terrifying, nothing to make a fuss about.
  • He accepted our fate like a stoic and refused to make a fuss.

Family gathering

All members of a family meeting together for a social event.

  • His recent death overshadowed the family gathering.
  • We're having a small family gathering to mark our wedding anniversary.
  • Think of it as singing at a family gathering.
  • Meanwhile, I can afford to be absent from family gatherings during many Chinese festivals.

Advertisement

A picture, short film, song, etc. that tries to persuade people to buy a product or service, or a piece of text that tells people about a job, etc.

  • She scanned the advertisement pages of the newspapers.
  • I wish to insert an advertisement in your newspaper.
  • I received an encouraging response to my advertisement.
  • The company misrepresented its products in the advertisement.

Sensible

Based on or acting on good judgment and practical ideas or understanding.

  • I think the sensible thing to do is call and ask for directions.
  • It would be sensible to take an umbrella.
  • There isn't a very sensible difference between a dog and a wolf.
  • She was reasoned into a sensible course of action.

Greeting card

A piece of thick paper folded in half with a picture on the outside and a message inside, that you write in and send or give to someone, for example at Christmas or on his or her birthday.

  • A group of nineteenth century greeting cards and valentines, types of ephemera now a popular field of collecting.
  • You've received a greeting card from a family member!
  • Anyone who buys this breakfast food gets a free gift of a fine greeting card.
  • I took a job at a place where they did greeting cards.

Annoyance

To make someone angry.

  • To her annoyance the stranger did not go away.
  • Polly clicked her tongue in annoyance.
  • A look of deep annoyance crossed his face.
  • I'm sorry that I caused you such annoyance.

Outrageous

Shocking and morally unacceptable.

  • I must apologize for my outrageous behavior.
  • It is outrageous that the figures are not in the public domain.
  • It is outrageous that these buildings remain empty while thousands of people have no homes.
  • Charges for local telephone calls are particularly outrageous.

Commodity

A substance or product that can be traded, bought, or sold.

  • Time is a very valuable commodity.
  • Coal is becoming a rare commodity.
  • Clean water is a precious commodity in many parts of the world.

Wages

The money earned by an employee, esp. when paid for the hours worked.

  • There are extra benefits for people on low wages.
  • He got a raise in his monthly wages.
  • Our company has decided to computerize its wages department.
  • The principal shall be obliged to pay the wages to the employees.

Fate

What happens to a particular person or thing, especially something final or negative, such as death or defeat.

  • We want to decide our own fate.
  • His fate is now in the hands of the jury.
  • Fate was strongly biased against him.
  • For every story of cold, the way is our fate.
