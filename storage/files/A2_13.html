Who, Whom, Whose

Who, whose, and whom can be used in questions or relative clauses.

Who is a subject pronoun like 'he', 'she' and 'they'. We use 'who' to ask which person did an action or which person is in a certain state.

In a sentence or clause, who will be used as the subject or to refer back to the subject in a sentence. In its simplest terms, this means who will be the part of the sentence or clause directly interacting with the main verb and performing the action.

Who wrote that letter?

Who is the manager?

Who is going to prepare dinner?

John, who got a new dog last week , needed to buy a stronger leash.

He got a new dog, one who barked all night and slept all day.

Simon and Danielle, who felt claustrophobic, refused to take the elevator.

I know the man who won the contest.

We knew the actress who starred in the movie.

Do you have any idea who he sold his car to?

We have two extra tickets for the concert. who wants to go with us?

It wasn't me! I have no idea who left the oven on.

Do you remember who received the Academy Award for best actress that year? Was it Nicole Kidman?

Whom' is an object pronoun like 'him', 'her' and 'us'. We use ‘whom’ to ask which person received an action.

In a sentence or clause, whom is used to refer to the object of a verb or preposition. Put simply, whom does not directly interact with the main verb or perform the action.

Whom are you going to ask to the party?

Whom did they choose as team captain?

Whom did they summon?

The lady to whom I spoke was French, not German.

The boys were exhausted, most of whom hadn’t slept in days.

James Madison, about whom we speak little nowadays, was a superb leader.

I don't know whom he invited.

Whose' is a possessive pronoun like 'his', and 'our'. We use 'whose' to find out which person something belongs to.

In a sentence or clause, whose is used to as a possessive pronoun. We use whose in a sentence to either indicate or to question who something belongs to.

Whose phone is this?

Whose is this umbrella?

Whose car is blocking the drive?

John, whose dog barked all night, is loathed by his neighbours.

Let him whose conscience is clear step forward.

I have no doubt whose mess it is.

Whose line Is It Anyway?

Whose camera is this?

She knew the family whose house we bought.

Whose dictionary is on the table?

Uses of Who, whom, and whose in Relative Clauses.

Who is used to introduce a clause giving further information about the subject - a person or people previously mentioned.

Whom is used in relative clauses to replace an object of a verb.

Whose replaces a possessive in relative clauses. It indicates who or what something belongs to.

SAMPLE SENTENCES EXPLAINATION
The woman who is standing there is a famous Youtuber. → who refers to the subject ' the woman'. Note: The dependent clause is: - The woman is a famous Youtuber.
The writer who wrote this book is my neighbor. → who refers to the subject 'the writer'. Note: The dependent clause is: - The writer is my neighbor.
Jane, who is my best friend, has just won the lottery. → who refers to the subject 'Jane'. Note: The dependent clause is: - Jane has just won the lottery.
The boy whom I met is a singer. → whom refers to the object 'the boy'. Note: The dependent clause is: - I met the boy.
The girl whom he adores died yesterday. → whom refers to the object 'the girl'. Note: The dependent clause is: - He adores 'the girl' .
The writer whom he described as talented has published a new novel. → whom refers to the object 'the writer'. Note: The dependent clause is: - He described the writer as talented.
The table whose leg is broken is in the kitchen. → whose refers to the possessive the table's leg. Note: The dependent clause is: - The table's leg is broken.
The house whose door is open is for sale. → whose refers to the possessive the house's door. Note: The dependent clause is: - The house's door is open.
The boy whose father is a karate champion is a genius. → whose refers to the possessive the boy's father. Note: The dependent clause is: - The boy's father is a karate champion.

Who and whom in informal English

While 'who' is is used as a subject pronoun and 'whom' is used as an object pronoun in conventional grammar, in informal English, who is used in both instances, that is as subject and object pronouns. 'Who' is thus used in informal English instead of 'whom' in contexts where the latter is typically used in formal English.

Whom did you ask? (Formal)

Who did you ask? (Informal)
