COULD + HAVE + PAST PARTICIPLE

Could have + past participle means that something was possible in the past, or you had the ability to do something in the past, but that you didn't do it. 

  • I could have stayed up late, but I decided to go to bed early. 

  • They could have won the race, but they didn't try hard enough.

  • You could have completed it sooner.

  • You could have blown your chance.

Use could have to talk about possibilities if something had been different in the past.

For example, someone who didn’t go to college can say:

If I had gone to college, I could have gotten a better job.”

When talking about a gymnast who didn’t win a competition, you can say:

She could have won the gold medal if she hadn’t fallen three times.”

Could have is often used with “if + had + past participle” (If I had gone / if she hadn’t fallen) – these “if” phrases express the imaginary past situation.  However, in some cases you can use have"   without the “if” phrase. 

Imagine you’re driving with a person who makes a dangerous maneuver on the road. You can say:

Are ou crazy? We could have gotten into an accident.”
