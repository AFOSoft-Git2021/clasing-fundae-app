Plural form of foreign origin’s nouns

Simple and quite rules of creating plural get a little complicated when it comes to words of foreign origin especially from classical Greek, Latin, French and Italian. 

Some words create plural by adding an ending -s, in others stand rules imported from original languages. 

1. Nouns ending with -us get an ending -i or -s:

Cactus

Any of many types of desert plant, usually with sharp spines (= long points) and thick stems for storing water

PLURAL FORM: cacti/cactuses

SAMPLE SENTENCES:

  • We drove to a neighborhood of neatly kept ranch-style houses, where cactuses and birds-of-paradise grew on freshly mowed lawns.

  • Succulents , such as cacti, store water in large parenchyma cells in swollen stems and leaves.

  • We drove to a neighborhood of neatly kept ranch-style houses, where cactuses and birds-of-paradise grew on freshly mowed lawns.


Fungus

Any of various types of organisms that get their food from decaying material or other living things:

PLURAL FORM: fungi/funguses

SAMPLE SENTENCES:

  • The clothing and gear of hikers and spelunkers can transport the fungus, according to scientists.

  • Scientists have found viruses in other species, such as fungi, plants, and insects, that help out their hosts.

  • Fungi are natural degraders of plant material and contain enzymes that biofuels researchers hope to harness for commercial applications.

  • Neither of these funguses should be gathered after rain, as they are then insipid and soon spoil.


Hippopotamus

A very large animal with short legs and thick, dark gray skin that lives near water in Africa.

PLURAL FORM: hippopotami/ hippopotamuses

SAMPLE SENTENCES:

  • Hippopotamus, giraffe, buffalo, cheetah, wild dog, and almost everything else has disappeared from much of their historical range.

  • The common hippopotamus can spend up to 16 hours a day immersed in rivers and lakes.

  • Crocodiles abound in these waters, as well as the hippopotami, or river horses.

  • There was every kind of animal, from wild dogs and porcupines to elephants and hippopotami.

  • Hippopotamuses and elephants have some of the sparsest, with just a few dozen hairs per square centimeter.


Octopus

A sea creature with a soft, oval body and eight tentacles (= long parts like arms)

PLURAL FORM: octopi/octopuses

SAMPLE SENTENCES:

  • If an arm is cut off, the octopus will not only regrow the arm, but the cut off arm can move on its own.

  • It seems the octopus would stop at nothing to catch its dinner, even some seaside crawling.

  • Giant Pacific octopi have huge, bulbous heads and are generally reddish-brown in color.

  • A few molluscs, like the slugs and octopuses, have secondarily lost their shells, having developed other means of protection.


Radius – (the length of)

A straight line joining the center of a circle to its edge or the center of a sphere to its surface: 

PLURAL FORM:  radii

SAMPLE SENTENCES:

  • Dispensaries may not be located next to a residential area or within a 1,000 foot radius of a school, park, library or any other dispensary.

  • Lottery officials say 22 percent of the lottery's video gambling money comes from bars and taverns within a 10-mile radius of the casino.

  • Due to the fast electron energy transport at radii smaller than 100–120 µm, the crater volume increases with the decreasing beam radius.


2. Nouns ending with -um get an ending -a or -s:

Bacterium

A type of very small organism that lives in air, earth, water, plants, and animals, often one that causes disease.

PLURAL FORM: bacteria

SAMPLE SENTENCES:

  • The bacterium that causes greening is hard to treat because it flourishes deep inside the tree, in its vascular system.

  • One bacterium can produce one million copies of the virus within four hours, so starting material isn't a problem.

  • Bacteria that live in the intestine convert food into various substances and one of those is gas.

  • Bacteria was introduced into his bloodstream through an unsterile needle.

Curriculum

All the courses given in a school, college, etc., or a particular course of study in one subject.

PLURAL FORM: curricula/curriculums

SAMPLE SENTENCES:

  • No doubt the report card is merely a reflection of larger shifts that need to happen, like curricula.

  • After the district adopted a new reading curriculum several years ago, the books were going to be trashed.

  • Since the publication of the landmark curriculum almost five decades ago, however, employers have completely changed their hiring standards.

  • Indeed, many business school professors have modified their curriculums to address issues in the financial crisis.


Medium

A method or way of expressing something.

PLURAL FORM: media

SAMPLE SENTENCES:

  • They told the story through the medium of dance.

  • While the medium has changed, the message remains: displaying red means you're serious.

  • The company was a pioneer in the market for streaming media to desktop personal computers.


3. Nouns ending with -on get an ending -a:

Criterion

A standard by which you judge, decide about, or deal with something:

PLURAL FORM: criteria

SAMPLE SENTENCES:

  • Pay is a very important criterion for job satisfaction.

  • Many schools regard prior education background as an important criterion and will not admit children educated in rural schools -- often considered inferior.

  • Measuring outcomes has been difficult because researchers, doctors and payers can't agree on criteria for success, he said.

  • The researchers found 47 studies with over 3,500 participants that met their criteria.


Automaton

A machine that operates on its own without the need for human control, or a person who acts like a machine, without thinking or feeling

PLURAL FORM: automata

SAMPLE SENTENCES:

  • I take the same route to work every day, like some sort of automaton.

  • Today the company keeps this tradition alive with its own automaton wristwatches.

  • There are cellular automata algorithms that allow for machines to learn, for sure.

  • On the contrary, noninterference in the lives of such automata would seem empty, for it would not express respect for them as persons.


4. Nouns ending with -ex or -vix get an ending -ices or -s:

Index

An alphabetical list, such as one printed at the back of a book showing which page a subject, name, etc. is on.

PLURAL FORM: indices/indexes

SAMPLE SENTENCES:

  • He got one of his research assistants to compile the index.

  • The thesis must include an index and a bibliography.

  • The major indexes closed out the week with losses of nearly 6 percent or more, their worst since 2011.

  • The bonds aren't added to the indexes investors are measured against until the end of each month.

  • In cases where several systems are analyzed, indices are also useful to make a comparative study between systems and to prioritize actions.


Matrix

The set of conditions that provides a system in which something grows or develops:

PLURAL FORM: matrices/matrixes

SAMPLE SENTENCES:

  • Europe is remaking itself politically within the matrix of the European Community.

  • The mechanical requirements were also important, because after polymerization, the matrix had to be stable.

  • One way to make big-data analysis computationally practical is to reduce the size of data tables -- or matrices, to use the mathematical term -- by leaving out a bunch of rows.

  • The observer facet did not account for significant variance in the simplest models fitting these matrixes.


5. Nouns ending with -is get an ending -es:

Analysis

The act of studying or examining something in detail, in order to discover or understand more about it, or your opinion and judgment after doing this

PLURAL FORM: analyses

SAMPLE SENTENCES:

  • Our financial experts conducted an independent analysis of the investment plan's performance.

  • I was interested in Clare's analysis of the situation.

  • The qualitative analyses focus on the learners' metalinguistic knowledge with regard to article selection.

  • Functionalist analyses of representations as making irreducible differences to behavior are piecemeal vindications of the scientific significance of old fashioned agent causation.


Diagnosis

A judgment about what a particular illness or problem is, made after examining it.

PLURAL FORM: diagnoses

SAMPLE SENTENCES:

  • Diagnosis of the disease is difficult in the early stages.

  • The tablets didn't help, and I suspected that the doctor had given me an incorrect diagnosis.

  • All the cancer diagnoses reported in the study were confirmed.

  • One of the overlooked ways is that diagnoses can change people's lives for the better.


Hypothesis

An idea or explanation for something that is based on known facts but has not yet been proved.

PLURAL FORM: hypotheses

SAMPLE SENTENCES:

  • Several hypotheses for global warming have been suggested.

  • Evidence supports their hypothesis that a long-term relationship built on trust and obligation between a buyer and a sub-contractor leads to prompt delivery and better cooperation.

  • The fundamental study hypothesis, based on prior work in animals, was that resveratrol supplementation would mimic the effects of calorie restriction.

  • These hypotheses are much beloved among liberals, atheists, and scientists.


Oasis

A place in a desert where there is water and therefore plants and trees and sometimes a village or town; an area in a desert where there is water and trees can grow.

PLURAL FORM: oases

SAMPLE SENTENCES:

  • One of my co-workers referred to me as "an oasis of calm in the midst of chaos".

  • The oases of serenity are the perfect destinations for relaxing travel, renowned for their natural beauty, abundant flora and walking trails.

  • Soaps, which offered the chance to be exposed to millions of viewers and the possibility of steady work, seemed like an oasis.

  • The goal was to make the rebuilt oases huge, bright atriums providing "destination" eating and shopping.
