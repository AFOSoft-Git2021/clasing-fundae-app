Mental Health

Everyone feels worried or anxious or down from time to time.

A few only know when it is a mental illness.

We hardly ever convince ourselves we are feeling these things we can’t explain.

What's the difference? A mental illness is a mental health condition that not only gets in the way of thinking, but it can affect relating to others, and day-to-day function.

Dozens of mental illnesses have been identified and defined.

They include depression, generalized anxiety disorder, bipolar disorder, obsessive-compulsive disorder, post-traumatic stress disorder, schizophrenia, and many more.

Mental illness is not only an exclusive nationality issue. It is an equal opportunity issue.

It not only affects the young but also the old. There should never question the gender, male and female can experience it.

Any individual of every race, ethnic background, education level, and income level can feel it too.

You should never feel isolated because it can often be treated.

Signs and symptoms of mental illness depend in part on the illness.

Under no circumstance, you shouldn’t invalidate other emotions that are not listed.

Here are common symptoms include:

  • feeling down for a while

  • extreme swings in mood

  • withdrawing from family, friends, or activities

  • low energy or problems sleeping

  • often feeling angry, hostile, or violent

  • feeling paranoid, hearing voices, or having hallucinations

  • often thinking about death or suicide.

In some people, symptoms of a mental illness does not only imply emotions but some appear as physical problems such as stomach aches, back pain, or insomnia.

Individuals with a mental illness should never feel alone. You can always seek professional help.

You should never keep the emotions to yourself.

Speak to your therapist about treatment plans you would like to take.
